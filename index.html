About me

I work with satellite remote sensing and machine-learning tools to make biodiversity models more ecologically meaningful and useful for conservation.

I am a PhD candidate and NASA FINESST Fellow in the Department of Forest and Wildlife Ecology at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, where I work in the SILVIS Lab with Prof. Volker C. Radeloff. My research bridges satellite Earth observation and ecological modeling to understand how biodiversity is distributed across the planet, what drives those patterns, and how species can persist amid rapid environmental change.

I am fascinated by a deceptively simple question: why do species live where they do?Answering it well has never mattered more. As habitat loss and a warming climate redraw the map of life on Earth, the models we use to predict where species occur increasingly decide where conservation happens. I work to make those models sharper, more transparent, and more honest about what they can and cannot tell us.

Much of my work develops machine learning approaches for species distribution modeling, with the broader goal of making biodiversity predictions more accurate, interpretable, and useful for conservation. In a recent study in PNAS, we showed that convolutional neural networks can improve species distribution predictions by learning the spatial context around each occurrence, rather than treating records as isolated points. This approach was especially valuable for rare and data-poor species, where traditional models often struggle.

Across these projects, my larger goal is to move species distribution modelling from simply mapping presence toward understanding population persistence. I aim to build models that not only predict where species are found, but also identify the habitats and processes that allow populations to survive, reproduce, and respond to environmental change. By making machine-learning models more ecologically meaningful, my work seeks to provide stronger tools for biodiversity conservation in a rapidly changing world.

Before Madison, I trained in geoinformatics in India and worked on hyperspectral images of Himalayan ecosystems, mapping species diversity and estimating forest and mangrove "blue" carbon. Across all of it, I care about methods that are both rigorous and usable, accurate enough to trust, and transparent enough to act on.

More detail lives in the publications and projects sections. For collaborations or questions, feel free to get in touch.

Projects

Research projects

Each project moves from an ecological problem, through a rigorous method, to a result that changes how we monitor and protect biodiversity.

Deep learning · PNAS

Teaching machines to map species better

Benchmarking convolutional neural networks against the leading presence-only species distribution models.

Problem
Most biodiversity data is patchy, presence-only records; classic models miss the complex spatial patterns they hide.
Method
Built and benchmarked CNNs against standard algorithms across many species using stacked environmental and remote-sensing layers.
Result
CNNs outperformed every other presence-only method — first-author paper in PNAS, enabling more accurate maps at scale.
Explainable AI · Ornithology

Opening the black box of bird habitat

Explainable AI for multi-scale habitat selection in birds.

Problem
Birds select habitat across many scales; ML models predict well but rarely explain why, limiting their use.
Method
Applied explainable AI to attribute predictions to specific environmental drivers and the scales at which each matters.
Result
A transparent, hierarchical picture of habitat selection that managers can translate into targeted action.
Satellite indicators · NASA

Satellite vital signs for biodiversity

The Dynamic Habitat Indices, from MODIS to Sentinel-2.

Problem
Global biodiversity monitoring needs consistent, scalable habitat indicators that link to where animals are.
Method
Co-developed the DHIs — cumulative productivity, minimum cover, seasonality — across MODIS, VIIRS, Landsat & Sentinel-2.
Result
Indices that predict bird abundance and richness, now used widely in biodiversity science and by NASA teams.
Hyperspectral · Carbon

Mapping fragile ecosystems & blue carbon

Foundational hyperspectral work in the Himalayas and Indian mangroves.

Problem
High-value, climate-sensitive ecosystems lack fine-grained maps of species diversity and carbon.
Method
Used hyperspectral & SAR imagery with machine learning to map diversity, biomass, and mangrove blue carbon.
Result
Multiple first-author papers establishing the remote-sensing foundation behind my current AI work.
